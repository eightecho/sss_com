Glossary & Quick Reference

Key terms, the six steps at a glance, the ecosystem, and the author.

01

Glossary

Key terms and named concepts used throughout the IMPACT System.

5D Creation Process
The five-phase production framework used in Productize: Define, Design, Develop, Deploy, Deliver. Applies to any product type regardless of format or delivery method.
Activate
Step 4. Responsible for building the always-on infrastructure that enables discovery, engagement, and conversion — Base Camp, strategic messaging, sales page, lead capture, nurture sequences, content channels, partnerships, and tracking.
Base Camp
The central owned hub where people go to learn about you, understand your offer, and take action. Typically a website. The destination all other channels point to. The only digital asset you fully own.
Campaign
Step 5. Responsible for creating and executing time-bound initiatives that drive measurable business results by applying concentrated pressure to existing infrastructure.
Decision Checkpoint
The structured decision at the end of every step: Proceed (move to the next step), Loop (return to a specific component to strengthen it), or Stop (the initiative isn't viable — reallocate resources).
Guiding Loop
The structural logic that makes IMPACT a compounding system rather than a linear one. Every Triumph feeds insight back into the next Identify. Each cycle starts with more knowledge than the last.
Identify
Step 1. Responsible for establishing the customer, the problem, the competitive context, and the strategic positioning that guides every subsequent decision.
Iterate
One of the three Triumph decisions. Choose Iterate when results are promising but below goal, specific bottlenecks have been identified, and there are clear hypotheses to test in the next cycle.
Monetize
Step 2. Responsible for establishing how value is created, measured, priced, and delivered in a way that generates sustainable revenue. Owns revenue model, pricing strategy, cost structure, and unit economics.
MVP (Minimum Viable Product)
The smallest, simplest version of a product that delivers the core transformation at the promised value. Designed to validate that the concept works before investing in refinement or scale.
Phase 1: Build the Foundation
Steps 1–3: Identify, Monetize, Productize. Establishes what you're selling, to whom, at what price, delivered how. Phase 2 depends entirely on Phase 1 being sound.
Phase 2: Generate Attention and Sales
Steps 4–6: Activate, Campaign, Triumph. The growth phase that connects the product to the people who need it and converts that connection into revenue.
Productize
Step 3. Responsible for building the actual deliverable that customers receive after purchase — defined scope, appropriate version, clear quality standards, and confirmed delivery infrastructure.
Scale
One of the three Triumph decisions. Choose Scale when goals have been hit consistently across multiple cycles, unit economics are positive and improving, and the model is validated sufficiently to justify increased investment.
Stop
One of the three Triumph decisions. Choose Stop when multiple iterations haven't improved results, the economics don't work even after optimization, or better opportunities exist that deserve your resources.
Triumph
Step 6. The analytical discipline applied at every level of the business — post-campaign analysis, step-level reviews, and recurring metric cadence. Owns performance analysis, funnel optimization, testing strategy, and the Iterate/Scale/Stop decision.
Unit Economics
The per-transaction profitability of a business: revenue per unit minus all costs per unit, including customer acquisition cost and the value of your time. Positive unit economics are required before proceeding from Monetize.
Version Ladder
The three-stage product versioning framework used in Productize: MVP (prove the core), v1 (refine the experience), v2 (optimize for scale). Each version builds on validated learning from the previous one.

02

Quick Reference

Step 1 Identify — Define the Opportunity Should this exist, and for whom?
Establishes customer, problem, competitive context, strategic insight, and positioning. Complete when you can articulate who this is for and why it matters with evidence — not optimism.
Step 2 Monetize — Establish the Offer Can this make money, and how?
Establishes revenue model, pricing strategy, cost structure, and unit economics. Complete when each transaction generates profit and the economics are defensible with real numbers.
Step 3 Productize — Create What We're Selling What exactly are we selling right now?
Builds the deliverable through the Version Ladder and 5D Creation Process. Complete when a paying customer could receive the product today.
Step 4 Activate — Get the Word Out How will people discover and engage?
Builds the always-on infrastructure: Base Camp, messaging, sales page, lead capture, nurture sequences, content channels. Complete when the infrastructure converts attention into audience and audience into customers — without manual intervention.
Step 5 Campaign — Drive Initiatives How do we apply pressure intentionally?
Executes time-bound initiatives that amplify infrastructure through coordinated effort. Complete when a full campaign cycle has been run with documented results.
Step 6 Triumph — Analyze and Decide What did we learn, and what do we do next?
The analytical discipline applied at every step and after every campaign. Complete when results are analyzed, bottlenecks identified, and a committed decision made: Iterate, Scale, or Stop.
The Decision at Every Step
Proceed — Step is complete. Move forward.
Loop — Something incomplete. Return and strengthen.
Stop — Not viable. Reallocate resources.
Phase 1 Complete When
You can deliver your product to a paying customer tomorrow and the transaction is sound — for both of you.
Phase 2 Complete When
You can generate revenue through campaigns and know with confidence what to do next.

About the Author

TADROBERT

I'm TADROBERT — a writer, strategist, and creative who has spent thirty years in advertising, marketing, and independent work as a freelancer and consultant, and about ten of those years trying to turn everything I learned into a system that actually makes sense.

My career started in advertising agencies in New York — building brand campaigns for companies like Quaker Oats, Novartis, and Hasbro. I moved into tech startups during the first internet wave, where I was recruited as Creative Director to build a marketing communications department from scratch. Then a corporate stint managing channel marketing for a major satellite brand. Then, since 2005, independent consulting — working with everyone from early-stage startups to established companies on marketing, communications, and strategy.

Somewhere in the middle of all that, I built something of my own — a self-funded SaaS product and a small team to deliver event marketing automation software. We built it, used it on client work, and were preparing to open it to the public. Then the 2008 market crash hit and shut us down.

It was a convoluted, expensive, and clarifying experience. We had the product. We had the clients. We had the intent. What we didn't have was a system — a clear, sequential process for building and launching something sustainable without the chaos, the wasted effort, and the decisions made in the wrong order at the wrong time. That experience set me down the path of Stupid Simple Startup. The IMPACT System is what I wish I'd had.

I'm building my first solo Stupid Simple Startup in public — a picture book project — which means you're not just getting a framework. You're getting a front-row seat to the process of building something real, making decisions with incomplete information, and improving systematically over time. The same loop this book teaches.
