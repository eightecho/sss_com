1
Identify+
Each cycle starts with more knowledge than the last

Linear frameworks assume you'll get everything right the first time. When you don't — and you won't — they offer two options: start over completely or give up.

The IMPACT loop assumes you won't get everything right the first time. It's designed for learning, not perfection. Each cycle corrects the cycle before it.

Linear systems exhaust. Loops compound. IMPACT is designed to compound. The Guiding Loop: every time you complete Triumph (Step 6), you feed insights, data, and hard-won understanding back into Identify (Step 1). Your second cycle starts with more knowledge than your first. Your tenth cycle starts with more than your second.

02

The Three Types of Loops

When you complete Triumph, your decision determines which type of loop you enter.

Triumph Decision: Iterate
Iteration Loop

Where you return: Usually Campaign, sometimes Activate or Productize.

Why: Results were promising but below goal. Specific bottlenecks identified. Core model seems viable but execution needs work.

Example: "Campaign converted at 2% instead of 4%. Bottleneck is sales page messaging. Loop back to Activate, rewrite the sales page with learnings, run another campaign."

Iteration loops are short cycles focused on specific improvements. You're testing hypotheses, not rebuilding foundations.

Triumph Decision: Scale
Scaling Loop

Where you return: Usually Activate, building more robust infrastructure.

Why: Goals hit consistently. Unit economics work. Model is validated. Ready to invest more resources.

Example: "Three campaigns hit 5% conversion consistently. Loop back to Activate to expand infrastructure, increase ad spend, build toward monthly campaigns."

Scaling loops involve bigger investment and longer timelines. You're building capacity, not fixing problems.

Triumph Decision: Stop
Fresh Start Loop

Where you return: Identify — new initiative.

Why: Current initiative isn't viable. Better opportunities exist. You've learned what you needed to learn from this one.

Example: "Four campaigns over 12 months. Conversion rate never exceeded 1% despite meaningful iteration. Market signal is clear. Stop. Return to Identify with a sharper understanding of what this market actually needs."

03

What to Carry Forward — and What to Leave Behind

Not everything from one cycle should flow into the next. Loop discipline requires distinguishing between insights and baggage.

Carry Forward
  • Customer insights — who actually bought, what language resonated, what objections came up, which channels converted best.
  • Economic data — real unit economics, true time investment, channel costs and ROI, pricing sensitivity patterns.
  • Infrastructure that worked — email sequences that engaged, lead magnets that converted, sales page structures that sold.
  • Strategic learnings — positioning that resonated, product-market fit signals, bottlenecks you hit and how you cleared them.
Leave Behind
  • Assumptions that proved wrong — you thought your customer was X, they turned out to be Y. Discard the wrong assumption, carry forward the corrected understanding.
  • Infrastructure that didn't convert — email sequences that didn't engage, lead magnets that didn't attract. Don't carry broken systems into the next cycle.
  • Products that didn't resonate — if Triumph revealed nobody wants this deliverable, don't force it into the next cycle. Build something different based on real demand signals.

04

Loop Discipline — The Five Practices

Understanding the loop intellectually is different from practicing it operationally. Loop discipline is the habit of completing full cycles, making real decisions, and feeding insights forward.

Discipline 1
Complete Full Cycles
One initiative at a time through all six steps. Reach Triumph. Make the decision. Then begin the next thing. Systematic learning only happens at Triumph. Incomplete cycles produce incomplete understanding.
Discipline 2
Make Real Decisions
Force the decision at every Triumph. Choose one of three paths. Document it. Commit to it. No decision is a decision to drift. Drifting consumes resources that could be deployed toward things that actually deserve them.
Discipline 3
Feed Insights Forward
Explicitly carry documented insights into the next cycle. Before making any Identify, Monetize, or Activate decision, review what prior Triumphs already know. Maintain a Learnings Library. Review it before starting any new cycle. The loop only compounds if the insights actually travel forward.
Discipline 4
Maintain Loop Velocity
Move deliberately and continuously. Finish Triumph, make the decision, begin the next step within days — not months. Insights decay. The longer the gap between Triumph and the next Identify, the more of what you learned evaporates.
Discipline 5
Respect the Sequence
Work through steps in order. Don't Campaign before Activate is functional. Don't Scale before the model is validated. Don't skip foundation work because it feels slow. Gaps created by skipping show up as downstream failure — usually at the worst possible moment.

05

The Compounding Effect

The real power of the loop reveals itself after multiple cycles. The system gets easier and more effective simultaneously — not because the work disappears, but because accumulated knowledge replaces guesswork.

Cycle 1
Discovery

Everything is new. The steps feel slow. You'll make mistakes at every stage. This is not a sign that something is wrong. It's the system working as designed. The first cycle's job is to complete the loop, not to be perfect.

Cycles 2–3
Evidence

You have real data from real buyers. You know which channels work. You have proven messaging to build from. The decisions that felt ambiguous in cycle one feel clearer now because they're informed by evidence instead of assumption.

Cycle 10+
Precision

The IMPACT framework is internalized. You identify opportunities faster. You validate or invalidate faster. You're operating from a body of knowledge that took years to build — and that nobody can replicate quickly.

This is what compounding looks like in a business context. The work doesn't scale linearly with cycles. The results do.

06

Common Loop Mistakes

Treating each loop as completely independent

You finish one initiative and begin the next as if you learned nothing. You're a beginner again every time.

The fix: Before starting any new cycle, review your Learnings Library explicitly. Ask: what do I already know that's relevant here?
Carrying forward everything — including failures

You keep using messaging that didn't work, keep building products nobody wants, keep investing in channels that don't convert — because that's what was built.

The fix: Distinguish between principles and tactics. Principles travel. Tactics get reassessed. What was built doesn't automatically deserve to continue.
Skipping Triumph because something new is more exciting

A campaign ends and you immediately start planning the next thing without analyzing what happened.

The fix: Triumph is non-negotiable. No new cycles begin until the previous Triumph is complete and documented.
Looping without improving

You run the same campaign structure repeatedly with the same messaging, same channels, same approach. Results don't improve because nothing changed.

The fix: Each loop must test at least one specific improvement based on Triumph findings. If you can't articulate what you're changing and why, you're repeating — not iterating.
Premature scaling

You hit your goal once, declare the model proven, and immediately try to scale aggressively. You skip iteration loops and jump to expansion.

The fix: Validate consistency before scaling. A pattern requires multiple data points — not one good result.
Never stopping anything

Every initiative lives indefinitely in a half-active state, consuming attention and resources without generating meaningful returns.

The fix: Practice Stop decisions. Set clear criteria in advance. If a defined number of iterations don't meaningfully improve results, stop — and mean it.
* * *
07

The Question That Guides the Loop

As you practice loop discipline over time, one question becomes the lens for every cycle:

What can I learn from this cycle that will make every future cycle better?

Not: "How do I make this campaign hit its goal?" — but: "What will this campaign teach me about messaging that applies to everything I sell?"

Not: "How do I build this product?" — but: "What will I learn about product-market fit that informs future products?"

Not: "How do I get this launch to work?" — but: "What does this launch reveal about my customer, my offer, and my infrastructure?"

This shift — from optimizing outcomes to optimizing learning — is what transforms IMPACT from a framework into a compounding system. You're not just trying to succeed at this initiative. You're building a knowledge base that makes future initiatives faster, sharper, and more accurate.

Every Triumph feeds the next Identify.
Your first cycle through IMPACT will be the hardest. Complete the full loop. Make the decisions. Document what you learn. Cycle 2 will be faster and more targeted. Cycle 10 will feel almost automatic.
That's the system.
