Phase 1 — Build the Foundation · Steps 1–3
Step 01 · Identify

Define the Opportunity

The first step. The hardest discipline.
"Should this exist, and for whom?"
01

Core Question This Step Answers

This is not about whether you're excited about an idea. It's about whether the world actually needs what you're considering, whether a specific group of people experiences a problem urgent enough to warrant solving, and whether you can articulate — with evidence, not optimism — who those people are and why this matters to them.

If you cannot answer this question with specificity and confidence, nothing else should begin.
02

What This Step Is Responsible For

Identify establishes the customer, the problem, the competitive context, and the strategic positioning that will guide every subsequent decision.

Identify owns:

  • Customer definition (who this is for, in observable behavioral terms)
  • Problem validation (what they struggle with, why it matters, what they've tried)
  • Competitive landscape (what alternatives exist, why they fall short)
  • Market context (forces, trends, timing considerations)
  • Strategic insight (the non-obvious understanding that creates advantage)
  • Positioning hypothesis (how this will be perceived relative to alternatives)
  • Validation plan (what assumptions need testing before building)

Identify is NOT responsible for:

  • Designing the product or service (that's Productize)
  • Determining pricing or revenue models (that's Monetize)
  • Creating marketing materials (that's Activate)
  • Running campaigns or driving sales (that's Campaign)
  • Measuring results or deciding what's next (that's Triumph)
The boundary matters. Identify answers "should this exist, and for whom?" with enough clarity that every subsequent step has direction.

When you skip Identify, you build for an imagined customer rather than a real one. You solve problems people don't actually have. You spend months building products nobody wants, then more months trying to market them to audiences who don't care.

Identify is not overhead. It is the difference between building a business and building a hobby that loses money.

* * *
03

Why This Step Exists — Failure Modes

Most business failures happen because people build the wrong thing for the wrong people at the wrong time. Identify exists to prevent this.

Failure Mode The "Build It and They Will Come" Delusion

The most common failure mode: assuming that because you can build something, someone will want to buy it.

You have a skill, so you build a product around it. You launch. You tell people about it. Maybe a few sympathy purchases. Then silence. Nobody buys. You don't understand why.

The reason: you never validated that anyone wanted it in the first place. This is not a quality problem. It's an opportunity problem. You built something well that nobody needs.

Failure Mode The "Validation Theater" Problem

The false confidence failure mode: performing research activities that look like validation but extract no real insight.

Social media complaints are performative. People exaggerate frustrations for engagement. They describe problems they have no intention of solving. People say they'd buy your service to encourage you — not because they genuinely see value.

Validation theater feels like Identify. It confirms what you already wanted to believe. It provides false confidence that leads to real failure.

Failure Mode The "Research Paralysis" Trap

The opposite failure mode: conducting endless research without ever committing to a direction. You interview 50 people. You read 30 competitor websites. You analyze 15 market reports.

But you're avoiding decision. You're delaying commitment. You're using research as a refuge from the vulnerability of choosing.

Identify is not about achieving certainty. It is about achieving sufficient clarity to proceed responsibly. Perfect information does not exist. At some point, you must synthesize what you know, make a judgment call, and move forward.

Failure Mode The "Skipping Straight to Building" Failure

The most expensive failure mode: treating Identify as optional and jumping directly to Productize or Activate.

You have an idea. You're excited. You start building immediately. Three months later you try to sell it. Nobody buys — because you never validated that anyone wanted it. You never defined who it's for. You built in a vacuum.

Now you have a finished product and no customers. And because you skipped Identify, you don't even know why it's not working. Skipping Identify doesn't save time. It guarantees waste.

04

The Eight Components of Identify

To answer "should this exist, and for whom?" with confidence, you need to understand eight interconnected elements. Each builds on the previous. Together, they create a complete picture of the opportunity — or reveal that no opportunity exists.

01 —
Customer
Who experiences this problem?

You cannot build a business for "everyone." Vague audiences lead to vague positioning, vague messaging, and vague results. Customer definition requires behavioral and psychographic specificity:

  • What do they currently do that reveals this problem?
  • What have they already tried that didn't work?
  • What constraints shape their decision-making?
  • What outcomes do they actually value?
  • What language do they use to describe their situation?
Quick Check

If you can't name three specific places where this person spends time online or offline, you haven't gone deep enough.

02 —
Problem
What struggle justifies a solution?

Not every struggle warrants a product. The problem must be:

  • Urgent enough that people actively seek solutions — not just complain occasionally
  • Painful enough that they'll pay to solve it — not just hope it goes away
  • Frequent enough that it's not a one-time annoyance
  • Concrete enough that you can articulate it specifically — not vague dissatisfaction
Quick Check

Can you describe the problem using the customer's exact words from interviews or observations? If you're paraphrasing or interpreting, you don't understand it yet.

03 —
Alternatives
What are they doing now?

Your competition isn't just direct competitors. It's everything the customer might do instead of buying from you:

  • Direct competitors — other products or services solving the same problem the same way
  • Indirect competitors — different solutions to the same problem
  • DIY solutions — figuring it out themselves via free content, trial and error
  • Doing nothing — living with the problem
Quick Check

Can you explain why each alternative fails to fully solve the problem? If not, you haven't found your positioning wedge.

04 —
Context
What forces shape this opportunity?

Market context determines whether you're building with the tide or against it. Forces to understand:

  • Trends: Is this problem getting worse (tailwind) or improving (headwind)?
  • Regulations: Are policy changes making this more or less urgent?
  • Technology: Are new tools making solutions easier or harder to deliver?
  • Economics: Are budgets expanding or contracting in this space?
  • Social: Are cultural shifts making this more or less important?
Quick Check

Can you name one major trend that makes this opportunity more viable now than it was two years ago?

05 —
Insight
What do you understand that others miss?

Insight is the non-obvious understanding that creates your strategic advantage. It's not a feature. It's a perspective. Good insight is specific and differentiated. Bad insight is obvious and useless.

Quick Check

If you shared your insight with a competitor, would they disagree — or say "obviously"? If it's obvious, it's not insight.

06 —
Positioning
How will this be perceived?

Positioning is not what you say about yourself. It's how customers perceive you relative to alternatives.

Strong positioning statement: "For [customer], who [problem], our [solution] is the [category] that [unique benefit]. Unlike [alternative], we [key difference]."

Quick Check

Does your positioning make it clear who you're NOT for and what you DON'T do? If it could describe anyone, it positions no one.

07 —
Validation Plan
What assumptions need testing?

You will proceed with assumptions. Validation is about identifying your riskiest assumptions and testing them before you build. Common assumptions to validate:

  • "People with this problem actually want it solved" (demand validation)
  • "They'll pay [price] to solve it" (willingness to pay)
  • "They'll trust me to solve it" (credibility validation)
  • "I can reach them through [channel]" (distribution validation)
  • "The timing is right for them to act" (urgency validation)

Validation methods: customer interviews (5–10 reveal patterns), landing page tests, pilot offers, outreach experiments.

Quick Check

Can you name the single riskiest assumption you're making, and describe a test that would cost less than $100 and take less than two weeks to run?

08 —
Opportunity Framing
What opportunity are we actually pursuing?

Before assessing viability, name the opportunity with precision. Not the product, not the solution — the opportunity itself. This framing becomes the lens through which all other components are evaluated.

Quick Check

Can you state the opportunity in one sentence that doesn't mention your product or solution? If not, you're solving before you've defined.

* * *
05

What "Good" Looks Like

Identify is complete when you can answer "should this exist, and for whom?" with confidence backed by evidence. The artifacts that should exist:

  • Customer ProfileBehavioral description specific enough that you could recognize this person if you encountered them.
  • Problem DefinitionClear articulation of what the customer is trying to accomplish, what prevents them, and why existing solutions fall short. Written in the customer's language.
  • Competitive LandscapeStructured analysis of alternatives. For each, you understand why customers choose it and where it falls short.
  • Market Context AssessmentDocumentation of forces, trends, and timing considerations.
  • Strategic InsightThe non-obvious understanding that creates your advantage. Articulable in 2–3 sentences.
  • Positioning HypothesisStructured statement of how this will be perceived relative to alternatives.
  • Validation PlanSpecific tests for your riskiest assumptions, with success/failure criteria defined in advance.
Quick Check

If someone asked "why should this exist?" could you answer in three sentences with specific evidence? If you need ten minutes to explain, Identify isn't done.

* * *
06

Common Traps and Misuses

Trap 1: Confusing Demographics with Behavior

Defining your customer as "women ages 25–40 with college degrees who work in corporate America" is targeting data for ads. It tells you nothing about behavior, motivation, or decision-making.

The escape: Define customers by what they do, not who they are demographically. Use demographics for targeting after you understand behavior.
Trap 2: Building for the Wrong Customer Segment

You identify three distinct customer segments, each with different problems and willingness to pay. You try to build one product that serves all three. You end up serving none.

The escape: Choose one segment as your primary focus. Build for them specifically. Expand to other segments after proving you can serve one well.
Trap 3: Mistaking Passion for Opportunity

You're passionate about a problem. You care deeply. This feels like validation. But your passion is not market demand. Your empathy is not proof of viability.

The escape: Validate that customers share your sense of urgency. If they acknowledge the problem but aren't actively seeking solutions, passion alone won't create demand.
Trap 4: Ignoring "Doing Nothing" as Competition

You analyzed competitors and are confident you can build something better. But you missed the most dangerous competitor: doing nothing. Living with the problem. Continuing as-is.

The escape: Understand why customers tolerate the problem. If the pain isn't acute enough to overcome inertia, even a superior solution won't sell.
Trap 5: Over-Relying on Expert Assumptions

You're an expert in this domain. You skip customer research because you already know. Expertise creates blind spots — you know the solution space, but may not understand the current customer's actual experience.

The escape: Validate even obvious assumptions. Your experience is an asset in building the solution, not a substitute for understanding the problem.
* * *
07

Decision Checkpoint: Proceed, Loop, or Stop

Proceed
  • You can articulate who this is for with behavioral specificity
  • You've validated that the problem is urgent and painful enough
  • You understand alternatives and can defend your differentiation
  • Your strategic insight is defensible — not obvious
  • You've tested your riskiest assumptions and they held
Loop Back
  • Your customer definition is vague
  • You can't explain the problem in the customer's own words
  • Your competitive analysis is superficial
  • Your positioning is generic — could describe competitors
  • You skipped validation because it felt unnecessary
  • Your insight isn't defensible when challenged
Stop
  • You can't find enough people with this problem
  • The problem exists but isn't urgent enough to motivate action
  • Alternatives adequately solve the problem
  • Market context creates insurmountable headwinds
  • Validation revealed your core hypothesis was wrong
  • You realize you're building this for yourself
Closing Principle: If you cannot clearly explain who this is for and why it matters, nothing else should begin.
* * *
08

How This Step Connects to the Next

Identify is the foundation every subsequent step builds on. The clarity built here — customer, problem, positioning, insight — carries through every step that follows. Monetize prices it. Productize builds it. Activate communicates it. Campaign amplifies it. Triumph tests whether it was right.

Feeds directly into
Step 2 Monetize

Monetize assumes you know who the customer is and what they value, you understand the problem well enough to price its solution, you've analyzed alternatives and know what price anchors exist, and you have clarity on differentiation.

Without Identify: You'll price based on guesses. You won't know whether to price high or low. You'll struggle to justify pricing because you don't truly understand what value you're delivering.

* * *
09

Workbook Handoff

The Identify Workbook forces you to commit to one specific opportunity. What the workbook will force you to decide:

  • Who exactly is this for? (Specific behavioral patterns)
  • What problem are they trying to solve? (In their words)
  • What have they already tried, and why didn't it work?
  • Who are you competing against, and why do customers choose them?
  • What do you understand that others miss?
  • How will this be positioned?
  • What assumptions are you making, and how will you test them?
Before You Proceed
Complete the Identify Workbook

There's no fixed timeline — your situation will dictate the pace. What matters is reaching sufficient clarity, not perfect clarity. A published answer that's 80% right beats an unpublished answer that's 100% theoretical.

If Identify feels easy, you're probably not digging deep enough. The discomfort is the work.

Do not proceed to Monetize until you can answer the core question with confidence: Should this exist, and for whom?
